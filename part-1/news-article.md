## GOES Ticketing Meltdown Leaves Thousands Empty-Handed as Platform Buckles Under Demand

*The platform processed fewer than 12% of attempted transactions before going dark — and fans are furious*

**By Rachel Okonkwo, Technology Correspondent**

When tickets went on sale for the North American leg of the Wavelength stadium tour at 10 AM Eastern on Friday, GOES's ticketing platform had one job. Within four minutes, it had failed to do it.

Fans attempting to purchase tickets were met with spinning loading indicators, error messages, and — for the majority — automatic session timeouts that dropped them from the virtual queue entirely. Social media filled within minutes with screenshots of failed checkouts, screenshots of the same seats "unavailable" after appearing as available seconds earlier, and a rising volume of pointed commentary directed at the platform.

"I had tickets in my cart. I could see the row and the seat number. I clicked confirm and got a timeout error. When I refreshed they were gone," wrote one user on X. The post accumulated over 200,000 likes by the end of the day.

By 10:08 AM, GOES had suspended the on-sale and issued a brief statement:

> *"We are experiencing higher than anticipated demand on our ticketing platform. We are working urgently to resolve the issue and will update on a revised on-sale time shortly. We apologise for the inconvenience."*

The revised on-sale was announced for the following Tuesday, adding fuel to a controversy that had already spread to entertainment media and beyond.

**A pattern, not an anomaly**

Industry observers were quick to note that Friday's failure was not an isolated incident. GOES's ticketing platform, launched in 2023 as part of the company's expansion into live events, has struggled with high-demand on-sales since its inception.

"This is a platform that was clearly not designed for the load patterns of major live events," said Anand Krishnamurthy, principal analyst at Beacon Technology Research. "The problem isn't that demand was high — everyone knew demand was going to be high. The problem is that the architecture couldn't handle a predictable spike that every competitor in this space has been engineering for since at least 2018."

Krishnamurthy pointed to how rivals handle similar situations: virtual waiting rooms, distributed inventory reservation systems, and auto-scaling infrastructure designed to absorb exactly this kind of concentrated burst demand. "The companies that do this well aren't magic. They've just invested in the engineering. GOES is years behind."

**The company's response**

GOES did not make executives available for comment, but the company issued a longer statement on Monday acknowledging "significant infrastructure shortcomings" and announcing a review.

The statement confirmed that the company has begun a "strategic technology modernisation programme" and that improving the reliability of high-demand on-sales is "a top priority." It stopped short of providing technical details or a timeline.

On Tuesday, the re-run on-sale proceeded without incident, though demand had significantly softened following the week of negative coverage.

**What it cost**

GOES has not disclosed financial figures related to the incident. Analysts estimate that combined primary and secondary market activity from a tour of this scale represents tens of millions of dollars in potential transaction volume. The reputational damage — particularly with artist management teams that choose their ticketing platform — may prove harder to quantify.

"Ticketing is a relationship business," said one industry executive, speaking anonymously. "If you fail a major artist's fans on an on-sale, you don't just lose that deal. Word travels."

The incident has prompted renewed calls, including from two congressional staffers in separate interviews this week, for greater scrutiny of ticketing platform reliability standards — a conversation that GOES almost certainly did not want to be having heading into the year.
